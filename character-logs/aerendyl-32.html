Aerendyl 32

Day 30 of Bloomweave, 515 T.A.?
As we ventured into the petrified expanse of the forest, my mind raced with possibilities. In the Earth Plane roam creatures capable of transforming their surroundings to mirror their own nature—Earth Elementals of various kinds, primal and barely sapient. I also recalled tales of settlements and caravans that had suffered the wrath of a Dao, left to stand as stone monuments to the folly of crossing such powerful beings. Yet this felt different, like some other force was at work here.

Amidst the grey, we sought a rumoured green presence, the same mystery that had drawn Corvaux's three trainees to this eerie place. Our path soon led us to petrified figures; not the trainees, fortunately, but others, frozen in time for a longer time still. One stood apart, hooded and twisted, a hand raised as if caught in the act of casting. The others, clad in identical armour bearing an oak leaf sigil, lay scattered, seemingly felled by a powerful blast.

Deidre's reaction was visceral—recognition and fear intermingled. These were members of a corrupt order from the old Autumn Court under Eristalon's rule, a group she knew all too well. As Mirri and Corvaux toppled one of the statues in an act of solidarity, I scanned the shadows, wary of unseen observers. The branches above, shattered like stone, hinted at a struggle of some sort. There was no real way of telling how recent it was, though.

Luck's attempt to detect magic yielded nothing, deepening the mystery. How could such widespread petrification occur without a trace of arcane energy? As I pondered this, the star's music, ever-present, began to clarify, almost as if intent on drawing my attention to it. The once garbled phrases took shape, speaking of creation once more, and … cosmic beginnings. Corvaux, too, heard the song—that is, its Celestial vein. We theorised that the star might be linked to the gods themselves, its melody a reflection of their influence, or perhaps the star sings of the creation of the constellation whence it came … Again: I have more questions than answers!

Our exploration then took us to a ruined castle, its collapse clearly postdating the petrification. The deliberate nature of the destruction, with bricks torn from the floor and scratch marks marring the stone, and Luck's particular investigation, suggested the work of a dragon. Deidre's knowledge of the place proceeded to paint a picture of a secluded base, seemingly used by the Autumn Court authorities to hide secrets from the public eye.

The age of the petrification must span a century or more at this point, yet the castle's destruction seemed more recent, with some areas having crumbled mere weeks ago. Not that this knowledge changed much; the recent damage could have been random, rather than the result of a stumbling dragon.

We proceeded farther down the corridor and down the hall, revealing a passage at the building's rear. This led us to a courtyard, where the impossible greeted us. Amidst the stone, living oak trees towered, their leaves rustling in defiance of the petrification. Ivy and moss, vibrant and alive, clung to the central building. Luck's magical detection revealed a chilling truth—the mist that enveloped this oasis was steeped in necromancy. Again. I thought it was mostly Winter that had a penchant for welcoming undead and the like, but I guess I was mistaken.

We prepared to brave the cold with that cold iron key in tow, dark magic permeating an adjacent chamber, but caution tempered our curiosity. As the day waned and Luck's exhaustion deepened, we chose to make camp, to rest and strategise for the challenges ahead.

I have since reached out to Wisp, my thoughts full of the star's ever-louder song. The chase must be nearing its end, but the true nature of my quarry remains elusive. Either way: time for some sleep now, before I must wake and join Mirri on the second watch.
