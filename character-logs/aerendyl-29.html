Aerendyl 29

Day 29 of Bloomweave, 515 T.A.?
Today was a day of gathering, both in supplies and revelations. We descended upon Master Silo's shop, procuring an assortment of potions and herbs. I bagged myself two pipes alongside a few doses of Dream Grass, as a treat: one made of quartz, resembling a dragon, and another made of wood, featuring two twined branches of sorts. Mirri even treated me to my dose of Druid's Dram.

But that was only my personal highlight of the day. Our towering host, Silo … something something IV, proved to be a kindred spirit in his proclivity for herbal relaxation, but more important than that was his stock beyond the remedies for our collective anxiety. Mirri sought a charm-repelling potion to no avail, so I bartered for vials of holy water, a necessity for a protective spell. Mirri's silver tongue and Deidre's unexpectedly pup-like eyes even helped secure a small discount—31 gold pieces per mixture, instead of 35. Truly, I thought Deidre's gaze could rival Grout's in terms of its capacity to endear …

But it was Luck's earlier find that turned out to be the most surprising find of all, really. A demon, trapped within a bottle, waiting to latch onto the nearest portal and tear open a rift to the Abyss. The 108th layer, if my intuition serves me right. A pattern I've grown uncomfortably familiar with. I'd like not to gain more hands-on experience with it than necessary, so I do hope he keeps that bottle firmly sealed.

Master Silo spoke of a necrotic frozen lake in need of cleansing, a task for another day. For now, we retired to the Slumbering Blossom, where Mirri opened up about Vestan Tlanbourn, the mage who's haunted her for 54 years—a span equal to my entire life. She painted a picture of a charming, manipulative caster, his true visage obscured by time and dark deeds.

But it was her own tale that struck me to the core. Vestan's ritual, the black ichor, the abyssal runes drawn onto ( or into ) her skin—a pact made manifest.

Mirri's pain was palpable as she recounted how Vestan took her brother's life, a mere 29 years of age. The weight of her family's scorn, the burden of survival—it all crashed over me like a tidal wave.

Perhaps the most chilling revelation to me, personally: her belief that my father, Malachai, might be one of Mystra's Children, a mage of unparalleled power born in the wake of the Spellplague's end.

Could he really have been chosen by Mystra herself? Literally?

No matter the answer to that question, a promise was due and so I made it—perhaps naive in my optimism, but fuelled by the unshakable faith in our group's strength and its burgeoning cohesion. We will see this through: Mirri's tale will not end in tragedy. Not on my watch.

As the night wore on, Mirri, Deidre and I indulged in some Dream Grass to upend the tension for a while, followed by a dose of Druid's Dram for Mirri and I not long after. Luck, meanwhile, succumbed to a potion-induced vision, a flying castle hurtling towards him … I think? Really, at that point, I was rather out of it, too. Mirri and I, lost in the haze of the tincture of sorts, heard the fungus sing, its joyous melody drowning out the star's ethereal refrain for the first time.

The giddiness that came with it all is slowly dissipating as I write this. Now to see if it is enough to permit a talk with Da'yin tonight …
