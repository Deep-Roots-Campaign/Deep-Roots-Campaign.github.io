Aerendyl 34

Day 1 of Flamecrest, year 515 of T.A.?
With the weight of Eristalon's violating presence still heavy upon me, I pleaded with my companions to flee the vault. The others understood the unspoken horrors that drove me, so we raced back through the chambers, using our dwindling supply of tokens to unlock the doors barring our escape.

But our path was not unhindered. Oh, far from it. Luck's shout pierced the air ahead of us, drawing our attention to the green vines snaking down the walls, grasping and seeking. The staff, the star—I feared they hungered for its power. Deidre's quick thinking conjured an illusory duplicate to draw their focus, and granted me a cloak of invisibility to slip past unseen.

I ran, my heart pounding in time with my footsteps, the sonorous draconic – ’thief,’ ‘fool’ – f my ears the moment I found the top of the stairs. The cold iron key, our salvation, opened another hidden door at the end of the hallway ( as our original point of entry was now populated with those same vines ).

I don't know the path everyone took afterwards. I took a sharp left turn from the door, and then another, intent on leaving the way we came through this path clear of vines, but …

Our flight was not without consequence. Deidre, meaning to collapse a wall and impede our pursuers I suppose, or intent on opening another passage for our escape, instead revealed a sight that froze the blood in my veins. A dragon, immense vicious, its form entwined with vines and lichen, its eyes blazing with fury and searching, searching, searching—for the thief who stole its treasure, the star it coveted, no doubt.

I took comfort in knowing it couldn't see me, but I worried for how long the spell's effects would hold, and whether the dragon could ‘sense’ the star, and locate it that way—and its new wielder along with it.

Luck, ever the brave negotiator, attempted to parley with the enraged guardian of this petrified hellscape. He offered a trade while I tried to escape, allowing me to somehow sneak below and past the dragon without alerting it to my presence. To help Luck out, I even dropped my precious Moonsickle as part of the tribute being demanded. But the dragon scoffed, demanding the staff, bemoaning the silence of the once-resonant song.

The dragon's wrath was swift and merciless. It lashed out at Luck and his loyal moorbounder, Ralph, leaving them grievously wounded. Yet, through sheer determination and skill, they managed to evade the worst of the onslaught.

Now, my guilt set in long before—the moment I realised this was all because the staff had been plucked from its glass cage. My companions were all fighting and putting on their best acts to distract the creature from me, so the staff and I could get to safety. I couldn't just leave them behind, though, and given Luck's and Ralph's state at that point … I relinquished the staff to Luck, and implored him to take Ralph and flee, and find safety.

As I turned back to rejoin my companions on the roof, a figure emerged from the shadows—dark hair, red robes, a stranger whose identity remained cloaked in secrecy. Luck's hurried ( but seemingly genuine ) assurances did little to quell the unease churning in my gut, but there was no time for questions.

So there I stood, facing the wall I had yet to climb with that red-robed stranger halfway up its height, with whatever fear I felt before under Eristalon's crippling influence amplified yet again.

In that moment, though, there was gratitude, too—for Mirri's understanding, for Deidre's cleverness, for Luck's bravery and sacrifice, and Corvaux’ steadfastness. They had risked everything to protect me, to safeguard the star, even in the face of what feels like certain death. I can now only cling to the hope that, together, we might weather this storm.
